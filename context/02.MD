The Path to a $100 Million AI Agency Exit
This guide summarizes the key concepts, frameworks, and lessons from Devin Kearns
(co-founder and CEO of Custom AI Studio) on building an AI agency positioned for a real
enterprise exit, not just a lifestyle business.
By: Nate Herk and Devin Kearns
I. The Big Picture: Why Now Is the Moment
The early majority of companies are finally coming online to AI. Recent model improvements
(Opus 4.6, Claude Code / Open Claude, GPT-5.5) have made AI accessible to "the normies,"
and executives are getting pressured by their boards to deliver a real AI strategy.
Key shifts happening right now:
● The value of pure development is trending toward zero. A 67-year-old lawyer can
now vibe-code a working app. Selling hours as "the developer" is collapsing as a business model.
● Org charts are consolidating. Yesterday: CEO + directors + large teams producing X
output. Tomorrow: CEO + a handful of directors + a flurry of AI systems producing the
same X output.
● The early majority is online. Leadership now has AI on their docket, and every
stakeholder is asking, "What's our AI strategy?"
● Skate where the puck is going. Continuing the same approach while assuming the
market stays the same will run you out of business.
II. The Shift: Lifestyle Business vs. Enterprise Value
Most people in the AI agency space are building a lifestyle business: a few clients, automations
for a couple grand, a comfortable income. That's a valid choice, but it caps your ceiling.
The alternative is building a sizable company with real enterprise value and a planned exit.
Custom AI Studio chose the latter and is gunning for a $100M exit.
Why this matters:
● Lifestyle businesses don't really sell. A freelancer or small SMB agency has minimal
exit value.
● Services firms rerate at scale, not category. Once you cross roughly $5M to $6M
annual revenue, the multiple you can command on EBITDA roughly doubles (from 1-2x
to 5x).
● Concrete example: A $2M/year AI consulting firm sells for about $2M (1x). A $6M/year
version of the same firm can sell for around $30M (5x).
● The rerating is exponential, which is why understanding the acquisition landscape
defines your strategy.
This path is not for everyone. Many people who say they want to start an agency don't actually
want sales, team management, or scaling. They want to be the builder. Both paths are
legitimate, but pick one and commit.
III. Why Mid-Markets Are the Sweet Spot
Devin argues mid-market companies (roughly $10M to $250M annual revenue) are the prime
target, more so than enterprises or SMBs.
The reason traces back to the most important pattern across 97% of Custom AI Studio's
projects:
Every AI project is fundamentally taking the logic of the business and converting it
into an AI system.
Why this favors mid-markets:
● They've already systematized. At this scale, they've had to hire people, write SOPs,
and build repeatable systems. That documented logic is the raw material for an AI build.
● They have clear KPIs and benchmarks to judge AI performance against.
● Enterprises are ironically less systematized. They throw bodies at problems and are
too distributed to execute a holistic strategy.
● SMBs and solo founders often run passion projects without a through-line to the
P&L. They lack the clarity to define success, so AI builds turn into scope-creep
nightmares.
Without an existing system, a clear KPI, and a benchmark, it's very hard to define success or
know whether what you're building will actually be valuable
IV. The Real Value: Logic Converted into AI Systems
A working example of the kind of ROI that justifies serious pricing.
The E-Commerce Refund Rate Case Study
The setup: A mid-market e-commerce brand processing 40,000 tickets per month, sitting at a
21% refund rate on their flagship product across 25 SKUs.
The economics of e-commerce:
● Win by outbidding competitors for ad placement
● That requires affording a higher customer acquisition cost (CAC)
● Which requires a higher lifetime value (LTV)
● Refund rate directly hurts LTV, so cutting refunds compounds across the entire flywheel
The client's ask: A 1% to 2% reduction in refund rate would mean millions of dollars to the
bottom line and put them on the path to exit.
The result: Custom AI Studio built a refund pushback system that brought the rate from 21%
down to 16%. That's a 4% to 5% reduction, well beyond the 1% to 2% target, with continued
improvement to come.
The flywheel this unlocks:
● Millions in working capital flowing back in
● More ad spend possible
● Better creative possible (which Custom AI Studio can also build at scale)
● Higher LTV/CAC ratio
● A real path to exit
Charging time-and-materials here would have been absurd compared to the value delivered.
This is what value-aligned pricing looks like in practice.
V. The 11 Playbooks for Making Money in AI
The 11 paths:
1. Creator (sponsorships, audience monetization)
2. Freelancer
3. One-on-one advisory consulting
4. Pure AI readiness consulting (assessments, audits)
5. Workshops, training, and enablement (e.g., enterprise Microsoft Copilot training)
6. Automations within n8n or similar
7. Managed agents (agent-as-a-service, licensing)
8. Vertical products
9. Horizontal platforms (the hardest play)
10. Hardware
11. Full-stack consulting firms (Devin's path) and private equity rollups
The full-stack consulting play goes up-market: heavy upfront consulting and discovery, market
analysis, ROI analysis, technical architecture, then building and maintaining the systems. This is
the path that resembles a McKinsey, Bain, or BCG for the AI era.
Important context: Anthropic recently launched a $1.5B AI enterprise services firm with
Blackstone and Goldman, going directly at the McKinsey model. Both Anthropic and OpenAI
understand that models will commoditize, so the trillion-dollar value lives at the application and
services layer.

The rerating effect (the key insight):

Most people don't have an exit number in mind. Devin recommends having one early because it
defines your strategy, your hiring, your offers, and the type of business you're actually building.
VII. The Customer Journey: Workshop → Blueprint →
Project → Partnership
You don't get a million-dollar contract from a cold intro call. You build the relationship through a
sequence of offers.
1. AI Workshop (Fixed Price, Entry Tier)
A two-session engagement to establish trust and a shared language.
● Session 1 (1 hour): Where the AI landscape is right now and where it's going
● Session 2 (2 hours): Industry-specific possibilities based on the client's pain points
By the end, you're positioned as the expert in the room on both AI and their business.
2. The Blueprint (Discovery Engagement)
The heavy consulting piece. Currently priced at $15K to $35K.
● Deep dive into how work actually gets done inside the business
● How data flows between systems
● Current cost to produce a given outcome
● First-principles redesign assuming AI is available
● Mapped to ROI
● Deliverable: a blueprint the client could in theory take to any dev shop
3. The Custom Project (Build)
One-time build, hand-off, team training. Scope is defined by what the blueprint surfaced.
4. The AI Technology Partnership (The Goal)
Inspired by the growth marketing partner model:
● Client provides a budget for the resources required to execute
● You take a percentage of topline revenue (or another performance metric)
● Aligns incentives, scales your value capture, and stops you from competing on
time-and-materials pricing that is trending to zero
Requirements for the partnership model:
● The right KPI with a clear through-line to the P&L
● A way to measure and attribute the impact
● A client whose business actually qualifies (not every business does)
The e-commerce refund example is the cleanest case: refund rate is the KPI, and the dollar
impact is directly measurable.
VIII. Productize Your Service: The Framework Approach
What separates a real consulting firm from "the kid next door who knows Claude Code"? A
productized methodology.
The trap: Saying "I'll build whatever you want" feels accommodating but actually erodes trust.
Clients don't feel you're standing on anything.
The fix: Build your own framework and lead with it. This is what McKinsey, BCG, Bain, PWC,
Deloitte, and KPMG all do. The framework teaches the client how to think about the problem
and positions you as the authority.
The shift in pitch:
● Old: "Yeah, I can build a sales automation for you."
● New: "I've developed a 5-step framework for converting business logic into AI systems.
Here's how it applies to your business."
You don't need to niche by industry if your process is niched.
IX. The Agentic Operating System Framework (The
Harness)
Custom AI Studio's framework is built around a harness architecture. The guiding principle:
If you were going to rebuild this operation from the ground up today, knowing what
AI can do, how would you do it?
Core architectural ideas:
● Event-driven system. Events are classified and routed to workflows.
● Workflows are as deterministic as possible. The harness orchestrates, not the LLM.
● LLMs are used surgically. They're called when reasoning, tool use, or unstructured
judgment is genuinely needed.
● Closed-loop learning. Every workflow has hooks at the end so the system can learn
and improve.
A useful reframe: AI vs. automation is not binary. The right question is, "What percentage of this
can be done with an agent, and how do we make sure that percentage runs every time?" A lot
of business clogs are fixed with simple deterministic scripts, not agents at all.
X. The QA Reality: Why Production Is Hard
Vibe coding has made building easy. Shipping reliably has not.
The risk of "dark code": When multiple people (and their AIs) work on a codebase nobody
actually reads, edge cases go uncaught and surface at scale.
The success criteria principle:
● Every scope module needs a real success criteria checklist, not one Claude generated
● The project manager or engineer must understand what "right" actually looks like
● A 1% error rate at 100 test runs is acceptable. At 10,000 production runs, that 1%
becomes 100 failures, which can collapse a business process.
● The margin of error compounds at scale, and "it's just an LLM hallucination" is not an
acceptable explanation
You need a deep enough understanding of architecture and success criteria to verify before
going to prod. Models will eventually self-verify better, but right now the human-in-the-loop on
QA is non-negotiable.
XI. What Clients Are Actually Buying
A reframe that should change every sales conversation: Clients are not buying an AI system.
They are buying relief.
What relief looks like:
● They can tell their board they have an AI strategy
● They can tell their employees
● They can tell their customers
● They can tell themselves
● The anxiety of "what are we going to do about AI" becomes excitement about getting ahead of competitors
The pressure is real. Executives are getting hit from every direction: LinkedIn posts from
competitors hyping AI wins, internal teams spinning up Claude Code, board members asking for
strategy. The market is flooded with noise, and nobody is giving a clear, coherent answer. (Or
rather, everyone is, which is the actual problem.)
Implication for sales conversations: Don't talk technical (RAG, vector DBs, model selection).
Talk outcomes and relief. Developers make poor salespeople because they default to "what"
and "how" instead of "why."
XII. Five Things Devin Wishes He Knew Sooner
1. Pick your endgame and commit. Lifestyle business or scaled exit, both are valid. Stop
hopping. Decide and run.
2. Productize early. The first 20 projects at Custom AI Studio were "build whatever you
want." Many of those builds aren't even in use anymore because they weren't packaged
around real value.
3. Charge what the value is worth. Time and materials is trending toward zero.
Performance-based pricing is only viable once your systems actually work, so build the
reputation first and then bet on yourself.
4. Build the funnel before you need it. If starting over, Devin would run a high volume of
AI workshops first, then layer in blueprints, then offers for custom development, then
partnerships. Relationships accumulate; you can't shortcut them.
5. Hire for the company you want to be. Cloning yourself doesn't scale a business. Find
people whose talents are different from yours: a strong sales partner, a detail-oriented
PM, an engineer who is better than you at vibe coding.
XIII. Bonus: Getting Started If You're New
If you're early in this journey and want to start making money, two things matter most:
● Distribution. Devin and Nate both grew on YouTube. Custom AI Studio didn't take off
until the channel did. Find the people who are willing to buy what you're offering, then
build a way to reach them consistently.
● Drop the imposter syndrome. You don't need a massive portfolio of case studies. You
need to understand someone's vision, show you can build it, and have genuine passion
and curiosity for the space.
A practical starter offer: charge friends and acquaintances $50 to sit with them for an hour and
teach them how to actually use Claude. Most of them know someone else who needs that too.
Final mindset note: AI is not its own bucket. In 10 years, "AI consultant" will just be
"consultant," because every vertical will have AI seeping into it. Whatever you're already
passionate about, find how to work AI into that passion. You'll already have the domain
expertise, which is the part that doesn't commoditize.
